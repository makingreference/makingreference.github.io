About the Project

Making Reference · History Minute

WWII stories told in about 60 seconds, blending cinematic visuals with true history — made for people who love the details.

What is History Minute?

History Minute is a short–form WWII storytelling project from Making Reference. Each episode is a one-minute story built from real events, real people, and real places — condensed just enough to fit a modern attention span without losing the humanity or the stakes.

The goal isn’t just to summarize the war. It’s to capture moments: a bridge held a few minutes longer than it should have been, a bomber crew making ice cream at altitude, a paratrooper who refuses to stay dead on paper.

How the episodes are made

Each story starts with research — books, articles, veteran accounts, and original documents where possible. From there, the story is written as a tight 60–75 second script, then broken into a sequence of frames designed to feel like a miniature film.

  • 8–10 cinematic frames per story
  • Focus on specific people, units, or moments in time
  • Grounded visuals: uniforms, vehicles, locations, and weather as accurate as possible
  • No “superhero” shots — just human beings in impossible situations

Why WWII?

World War II is endlessly studied, but still full of corners, side stories, and quiet acts of courage that rarely make it into textbooks. This project exists for the forgotten operations, the “oh, I never knew that” missions, and the tiny decisions that changed the course of huge events.

Where to watch

New episodes are released primarily as vertical video — built for phones, but detailed enough to hold up on a big screen.

Behind Making Reference

Making Reference is a one-person shop: researching, writing, and assembling each story episode by episode. The goal is simple: make history feel close enough that you can almost smell the exhaust, the cordite, the coffee in the tin mug.

If you have a family story, a unit, or a specific moment you think deserves an episode, you’re invited to reach out.
